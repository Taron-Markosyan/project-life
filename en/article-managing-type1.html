Managing Type 1 Diabetes

Managing Type 1 diabetes is a daily process, but with the right habits and tools, it becomes a natural part of everyday life. Below are the main components of management.

Three Main Components

Insulin therapy — since the body cannot produce insulin on its own, it must be supplied from outside, through injections (with a pen or syringe) or an insulin pump. The dose is determined together with a doctor, taking into account food, activity level, and blood sugar readings throughout the day.

Glucose monitoring — regularly measuring blood sugar (with a glucometer or a continuous glucose monitoring sensor, CGM) allows you to respond in time to high or low readings and adjust your insulin dose.

Carbohydrate counting — accurately estimating the amount of carbohydrates in food allows you to match your insulin dose to your meal. Read more about this in the Nutrition and Bread Units section.

Recognizing Low and High Blood Sugar

Signs of low blood sugar (hypoglycemia) include shakiness, sweating, dizziness, and confusion — in this case, it's important to immediately consume fast-acting carbohydrates (such as fruit juice). Signs of high blood sugar (hyperglycemia) include intense thirst, frequent urination, and fatigue, which may require an additional insulin dose as directed by your doctor. Always keep a source of fast-acting sugar on hand.

Everyday Habits

  • Regular, evenly spaced meals
  • Accounting for the effect of physical activity on blood sugar levels
  • More frequent checks during illness or periods of stress
  • Regular visits with a doctor or diabetes education specialist
