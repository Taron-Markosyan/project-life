What is Type 1 Diabetes

Type 1 diabetes is a chronic autoimmune condition in which the immune system mistakenly destroys the beta cells of the pancreas — the cells that produce insulin. Without enough insulin, the body can't use glucose (sugar) for energy, and blood sugar levels rise.

Why It Happens

The exact cause of Type 1 diabetes is still unknown. It's thought that a genetic predisposition combined with certain environmental factors (such as viral infections) may trigger the immune system's faulty response. It's important to understand that Type 1 diabetes is not related to diet or lifestyle and cannot be prevented — this is what sets it apart from Type 2 diabetes. It can develop at any age, though it's most often diagnosed in childhood and adolescence.

Main Symptoms

  • Intense thirst and dry mouth
  • Frequent urination
  • Unusual fatigue and weakness
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Blurred vision

Symptoms usually develop quickly, over a few days to a few weeks.

How It's Diagnosed

To diagnose Type 1 diabetes, a doctor relies on tests that measure blood sugar levels, including a fasting blood glucose test and an HbA1c test, which shows the average sugar level over the past 2–3 months. Additional tests may be done if needed to confirm the specific type of diabetes.

Life With Type 1 Diabetes

Type 1 diabetes is a lifelong condition that requires daily insulin therapy. However, with proper management, people living with diabetes can lead full, active, and healthy lives. Read more in the Management section.
